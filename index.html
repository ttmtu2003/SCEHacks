    Overview of Housing Crisis in California

    The current housing market in California is similar to how it was prior to COVID. Sale prices in many regions have been rapidly increasing within the past few years. Over last year, median sale price increased by $100,000, averaging above $800,000 in areas such as Irvine, Fremont, Oxnard, Elk Grove, and San Jose.

    Three housing crises within California

    The Large Number of Homeless Californians

    Homelessness in California is surging and is higher than all other states combined, with the rate of deaths on streets reaching 32% last year. In 2020 alone, 27.89% of California citizens experience homelessness. This percentage translates to about 161,548 people. Some prominent causes of homelessness include a traumatic event, loss of a job, the inability to pay for needed health care, or a criminal background got in the way of finding a job.

    Californians Facing Poverty

    The California Poverty Assessment Report, which takes into account the cost of housing and the cost of living, shows that 17.8% of Californians are living below the basic standard of living, which is equivalent to 7.1 millions of Californians. While the wages remain the same, the cost of housing skyrockets, thus, contributing to 56% of the cause of poverty. The situation is even worse for Black and Brown renters who face intense displacement and gentrification pressures, live in overcrowded and unsafe housing conditions, and have fled urban cores for cheaper exurbs over the past two decades.

    What Is Being Done

    The California state government has been working on ways to reduce the cost of housing. As of September 28, 2021, Governor Newsom has approved 31 affordable housing bills. Through these signed bills, Newsom reduces the amount of red tape and ensures cities provide enough housing. Newsom also dedicated 22 billion dollars to the California Comeback Plan, an action that will enable the creation of more than 84,000 housing units and opportunities for the homeless to find homes.

    As a part of the signed bills, a new division called Housing Accountability Unit will be a part of the California Department of Housing and Community Development to provide the necessary support to create more housing at the local level.

    Organizations Dedicated to the Cause

    People Assisting The Homeless (PATH)

    PATH works to end homeless for all Californians by building affordable homes and offering services to support those who are affected. This organization works in Los Angeles, the Central Coast, San Diego, and the Bay Area.

    Find out more information about the organization and ways to help here!

    Coalition on Homelessness

    Coalition on Homelessness brings homeless people and front line service providers together to discuss viable, lasting solutions to homelessness in San Francisco. Some of their accomplishments include assisting the end of the Matrix program, a program that harmed the homeless population, and successfully convinced the San Francisco Housing Authority to house 300 homeless families in previously vacant housing units.

    Food on Foot

    Food on Foot helps those who are homeless or receive low-income in Los Angeles by providing food, clothes, and an opportunity to start anew. This organization allows these people to have a clean slate by training them to obtain life-skills, helping them achieve stable jobs for their lifetimes, and finding them a safe and permanent housing situation.
